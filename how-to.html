How to use this site:

This site was developed and tested using Chromium Browser (Google Chrome). If something looks or acts a bit off, it might be because your bowser.

Navigation:

Each of the options menus can be closed and opened by clicking on the bars on the edges of the window like the one below.

Zoom:

Click to zoom and center on a geography. To get a closer look, click on a smaller geography; the map scales proptionally to the area of the geography. You can recenter the map by clicking the same geography twice or by clicking on the background of the map.

Using your onwn data:

This site allows you to open any additional data that you’d like to explore that isn’t already in the dataset. These files will not be saved or uploaded to a severe anywhere. To prevent data loss due to differences in data types and handling accross software and languages, I suggest saving all of you data (at the very least the geographic identifiers) that you intend to use as UTF-8 encoded character stings before attempting to load it.
Varibles should not be stored as null unless you expect them to be treated as zeros or they are stored as a character sting and you expecte them to treated as missing values. Variables in this form have not been rigously tested; you may risk data loss and failed or incorrect merges.
If any of your variables have the same name as a variable in the EoO dataset, the EoO data will be overwritten.
  1. Click the top navigation bar at the top of the page, above the map—a dropdow box will open.
  2. Click
    Choose a .csv
    . Your machine will open a dialog box and allow you choose a file that will open in your machine’s memory.
  3. Once the file is succesfully openend, the rest of the menu will be become active. Click on the selector at the top of the third column and choose the varible in your dataset that uniquly matches the geographic identifier in the Equality of Opportunity dataset. It must be a contatenation of the 2000 two-digit state FIPS and three-digit county FIPS codes with leading zeros.
    If you are loading data with missing geographic identifies, they should be stored as NA or NaN.
  4. Upon completion, you should see some information about the sucess rate of the merge.
    There are two ways for your merge to partially or completely fail: your data contains codes not found in Equality of Opportunity data or vice versa.
    If your merge partially (or completely) failed, you can download a .json file that the unmatched observations from your data set and the identifiers of the unmatched observations from the EoO data. A generalized exmaple of the file structure is below:
    {unmatched_cli:
    [{var1: observation1, var2: observation1, ... varK: observation1},
    {var1: observation2, var2: observation2, ... varK: observation2},
    ... ,
    {var1: observationN, var2: observationN, ... varK: observationN}],
    unmatced_serv:
    [{GEOID: observation1, ST: observation1, COUNTY: observation1},
    ... ,
    {GEOID: observationN, ST: observationN, COUNTY: observationN}]
    }
  5. If your merge was patially (or completely) successful, you’ll be able to export the data as a .csv
    (At the very least, this site can be used as interactive tool to merge data.)
You will now be able to use any of the mapping, plotting, regression table, and monotonic transformation features on your data.
Multipule datasets can be opend and merged as long as they don’t have the same varible names of the EoO data or your previously loaded files.
